Hunting for the particles hiding in plain sight.

I'm a PhD researcher based in Budapest, building and testing detector technology for one of the experiments at CERN. My work sits somewhere between a physics puzzle and a hardware workshop — part of it is figuring out what an invisible particle might look like if we could ever catch one, and part of it is soldering, testing, and calibrating the equipment that gives us a chance to.

Who I am
About Me

I grew up in Kerala, India, and studied physics through an integrated Bachelor's and Master's program at the Central University of Tamil Nadu. Along the way I got hooked on a simple question: what is the universe actually made of? Not the 15% we can see and touch — the rest of it.

That question took me to IIT Mumbai, working with the ALICE experiment team on how particles fly apart after tiny, violent collisions, and now to Eötvös Loránd University (ELTE) in Budapest, where I'm a PhD scholar building detector components for the LHCb experiment at CERN, near Geneva.

I like problems that make you work with your hands as much as your head — a good day for me involves a whiteboard in the morning and an oscilloscope in the afternoon.

What I study
Chasing the Invisible

About 85% of the universe is made of something we call dark matter — we know it's there because of how it pulls on everything around it, but no instrument has ever directly caught a piece of it. My work is aimed at building the kind of detector that might.

Some particles may be so shy that they pass straight through ordinary detectors, travel for meters, and only reveal themselves far from where they were born.

HardwareBuilding the eyes of a detector

At ELTE, I run an optical test bench that checks the light sensors (PMTs) destined for LHCb's upgraded calorimeter — making sure each one responds fast enough, consistently enough, and quietly enough to be trusted. I also help quality-check the absorber modules and fibres that make up the detector itself, coordinating with teams at CERN so everything is ready ahead of installation.

SimulationModelling what a hidden particle would look like

I also work on CODEX-b/β, a proposed detector designed to sit a short distance from LHCb's collision point and catch particles that live just long enough to sneak past the main experiment. I build simulations that ask: if such a particle existed, what trace would it leave behind — and how would we recognise it as something new rather than noise?

Research Timeline

PhD Scholar

Eötvös Loránd University (ELTE) · Budapest, Hungary — Supervisor: Prof. Biplab Dey

Testing and qualifying the light sensors and detector modules for LHCb's next-generation calorimeter.

  • Built an optical test bench to check timing, consistency, and background noise in the detector's light sensors, in step with other testing labs worldwide
  • Quality-checked absorber modules and optical fibres ahead of installation during the LHC's next shutdown

Project Associate

Indian Institute of Technology Bombay · Mumbai, India — Supervisor: Prof. Sadhana Dash

Studied how particles fly apart after high-energy collisions, as part of India's contribution to the ALICE experiment at CERN.

  • Ran the first computer simulations of a particular statistical pattern in proton collisions, to help interpret future real data
  • Looked at how the choice of particles studied and the detector's field of view can bias that pattern
